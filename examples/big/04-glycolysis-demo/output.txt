The **Teusink Yeast Glycolysis Model**, originally developed by Jana Wolf and collaborators, investigates the dynamics of glycolytic oscillations in the yeast *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*. Here’s an overview of the key aspects of the model:

### Model Overview

- **Title**: Wolf2000 Glycolytic Oscillations
- **Description**: The model captures the oscillatory dynamics of key metabolites during glycolysis, particularly ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate) and NADH (Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide), showcasing how these concentrations change over time in yeast cells.
- **Purpose**: The model illustrates how glycolytic oscillations can synchronize among a population of yeast cells, mediated through acetaldehyde.
  
### Publication Information

- **Journal**: Biophysical Journal
- **Year**: 2000
- **Link**: [Read the original paper](http://identifiers.org/pubmed/10692304)
- **Authors**: Includes prominent researchers like Jana Wolf and H.V. Westerhoff.

### Key Dynamics and Findings

1. **Glycolytic Oscillation**: The model demonstrates how glycolysis, the pathway for glucose metabolism, can produce oscillations in ATP and NADH levels, driven by the underlying biochemical reactions.
  
2. **Synchronization Signal**: It explores the transmission of dynamics through intercellular signaling, specifically relating to acetaldehyde production and propagation.

3. **Modeling Approach**: The model employs ordinary differential equations (ODEs) to represent the dynamic behaviors of metabolites over time.

### Simulation Outcomes

I ran a brief simulation of the model over 100 time units, and here are some notable results:

- **ATP Concentration**: Fluctuates between approximately 1.6 to 3.0 units, showcasing periodic oscillations characteristic of the glycolytic cycle.
- **NAD Concentration**: Similarly exhibits oscillatory behavior, starting at around 0.6 and adjusting through its metabolic pathways.
- **Acetaldehyde**: Shows minor fluctuations in concentration, indicating its role as both a product and a signaling molecule during glycolysis.
